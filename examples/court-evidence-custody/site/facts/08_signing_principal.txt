WHY THE PRINCIPAL IS ROLE-LEVEL, NOT PERSON-LEVEL

In real-world digital-forensics practice, the binding between a
forensic finding and the person who produced it is established
through:
  - the examiner's contemporaneous lab notes,
  - the examiner's CV / expert-witness disclosure under FRCP 26 (or
    state equivalent),
  - and the examiner's live testimony at any evidentiary hearing.

A public Bitcoin-anchored seal is not the appropriate venue to bind
an examiner's personal identity to a case. That binding belongs in
the court record, where access is controlled and where the binding
can be tested through cross-examination. The role-level public seal
plus the separately-discoverable role-to-individual binding (hashed
in FACT 05) is the kit's default; agencies in jurisdictions where
public personal-naming is required can amend their fork to include
the personal name, but the default refusal favors examiner safety.

In a real attestation, the signing principal section should include:

  - The acquiring agency's identity and statutory basis.
  - The role and supervisor-role for each signatory.
  - A cryptographic key (PGP, X.509, agency-issued) so that future
    statements claimed to be from the signing principal can be
    cryptographically verified against this seal.
  - The acquisition and sealing timestamps.

The signing principal section SHOULD NOT include:

  - Personal contact information for examiners or supervisors.
  - Home addresses or other locating information.
  - Identifying information about examiners' family members.
  - The name of any private party to the investigation
    (suspect, defendant, victim, witness, premises occupant).

This fact asserts only the signing principal identification fields
for the fictional acquisition as declared. It does not assert any of
those fields about any real agency, any real examiner, or any real
case.
