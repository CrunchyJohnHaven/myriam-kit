ACQUISITION VERIFICATION PROCEDURE

  For each imaged item, after imaging completed:
    1. The imaging tool computed both MD5 and SHA-256 hashes of the
       acquired image during the imaging pass.
    2. A second pass (verification read) re-hashed the image and
       compared to the first-pass hash.
    3. For live-source acquisitions where the source device could be
       re-read non-destructively, the source device was re-hashed
       after imaging and compared.
    4. All three hashes (image-1st-pass, image-2nd-pass, source-
       device-post-image) matched for Items 1, 2, 4, and 6.
    5. For Items 3 (phone) and 5 (body-cam), only the image-level
       hash is meaningful; the source-device hash is not reproducible
       for those acquisition types.
    6. The hashes recorded in FACT 03 are the SHA-256 image hashes
       after verification.

  Verification log hash (sealed separately, contains per-item details):

FORENSIC IMAGE FORMAT

  - Items 1, 2, 4, 6: EnCase Evidence File format (.E01), variable
    chunk size, compression on, MD5+SHA-256 embedded.
  - Item 3: AFF4 (Advanced Forensic Format v4), GrayKey native
    output format.
  - Item 5: bytewise-preserved TAR archive of the original .mp4
    bodycam files plus the BWC vendor's manifest .json. Per-file
    hashes of the .mp4 contents are stored INSIDE the TAR's manifest
    .json, NOT in this public manifest.

WHAT THIS FACT DOES NOT CERTIFY

  - That the tools above are bug-free. FTK Imager, GrayKey, and AFF4
    parsers have all had documented bugs in past versions. The
    sealed version-and-binary-hash record allows later parties to
    cross-reference against the vendor's published vulnerability and
    bug-fix lists.
  - That the write-blockers performed correctly on the specific
    media in question. The NIST CFTT protocol verifies the general
    case; specific-media edge cases (e.g., hybrid SSHD platters,
    certain SSD wear-leveling firmware) are known to behave
    differently and the verification protocol does not catch all
    such edge cases.
  - That the acquisition procedure was the BEST procedure available;
    only that it was the procedure followed. Methodology choices
    are subject to Daubert challenge under FRE 702 in U.S. federal
    courts and similar standards elsewhere.

This fact asserts only the acquisition tools, write-blocker hardware,
verification procedure, and image format used for the fictional
acquisition. It does not assert that the methodology was valid under
any specific court's admissibility test, that the tools were free of
defects, or that the resulting images are necessarily an exact
representation of the source devices' contents in every possible
edge case.
