Each hash was computed with SHA-256 over the exact bytes of the
forensic image file as written by the imaging tool, immediately after
imaging completed and BEFORE any examination began. A second hash
(sealed separately in FACT 04 as part of the methodology record)
verifies the image against the live source device at acquisition time.

Any party — defense expert, opposing counsel, appellate-review forensic
examiner, court-appointed neutral — receiving any of the above
forensic-image files through proper discovery may re-hash the file
they receive with SHA-256 and compare to the row above. If the hashes
match, the recipient holds the exact bytes that were sealed at the
moment of acquisition. If the hashes do not match, the file has been
altered (intentionally or accidentally) since acquisition; the
recipient should raise this immediately with the issuing agency and
opposing counsel.

In this fictional example, the hashes above are deterministic
placeholders. A real acquisition would replace these with hashes of
the actual forensic images.

What is INTENTIONALLY ABSENT from this manifest:

  - File-by-file hashes of individual files INSIDE the disk images.
    Those reside in the examination report, hashed separately in FACT
    05's custody log, NOT in this public manifest. The reason: an
    individual file's hash on a suspect's personal device may itself
    be evidence relevant only to the prosecution and defense, and
    public hashing creates a permanent searchable record that could
    re-identify the case subject via correlation with other databases.
  - Any file-level hash of suspected child sexual abuse material,
    biometric template, or other PII-equivalent data. Such material
    is NEVER hashed into this kit's public manifest, even by hash;
    see refusal-rule R3 in FACT 07.
  - Metadata that would identify any person at the premises: account
    names, user account UIDs, phone-book entries, biometric
    enrollment IDs, etc.

This fact asserts only the manifest of forensic-image-level hashes,
sizes, formats, source devices (anonymized), and acquisition methods
above, and the integrity property the hashes provide. It does not
assert:
  - what evidence the images contain,
  - whether the contents are incriminating or exculpatory,
  - whether the images were acquired within warrant scope,
  - whether the source-device chain-of-custody before imaging was
    proper (covered separately in FACT 05),
  - whether the examination of these images was methodologically
    sound (covered separately in FACT 04 and 06).
