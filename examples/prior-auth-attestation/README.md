# Prior-Authorization Attestation Kit

A turnkey myriam-kit example for U.S. health insurers (medical directors,
utilization-review physicians), provider organizations (hospital UM
teams, large practices, IPAs), state insurance regulators, and patient
advocacy organizations who want to publish a Bitcoin-anchored,
tamper-evident seal of a prior-authorization decision at the moment the
decision is rendered — so that the criteria the insurer claims to have
applied, the evidence the provider claims to have submitted, the
appeal-window the patient was given, and the dollar value and reason
code on the decision are all permanently fixed at the moment of decision
and silent revision by either side becomes cryptographically detectable.

The seal is designed to be **diffable against future denial or approval
records, against future appeal decisions, and against future "we always
applied criteria X" claims**. A single sealed prior-auth attestation is
useful in a single dispute; the public record of an insurer sealing
every prior-auth decision under the same criteria-version disclosure is
the load-bearing artifact for systemic gaming detection (silent criteria
revision, AI-denial-model rollouts without physician review, inconsistent
denial-then-approval patterns across identical cases).

---

## What this kit DOES NOT do (read first)

The seal is a cryptographic timestamp of WHICH prior-authorization
request was received, WHICH clinical criteria (with version + revision
date) the insurer cited as the basis for the decision, WHICH evidence
the provider submitted (by hash, not content), WHAT the decision was
(approve / partial / deny / pend) with reason code and dollar value,
WHAT appeal window the patient was given, and WHO the licensed signing
principal was. It cannot answer any question about whether the decision
was *correct*. The following are **outside the seal's domain**:

1. **Compelling the insurer to cover the treatment.** A sealed denial
   is still a denial. The seal does not approve care, override the
   insurer's contract, or create a coverage obligation that did not
   exist under the policy. The patient's recourse remains internal
   appeal, external review (state-mandated IRO or federal IRO),
   state insurance department complaint, ERISA private right of
   action where applicable, or court. The seal makes those processes
   *forensically cleaner*; it does not replace them.

2. **Validating the clinical criteria themselves.** The seal records
   that the insurer cited Milliman Care Guidelines edition X chapter
   Y subsection Z (or InterQual SIM version A criterion B, or the
   carrier's internal medical policy MP-12345 revision 7). It does
   NOT assert that those criteria are medically correct, that they
   reflect current standard of care, that they were appropriate for
   this specific patient's clinical presentation, or that the
   criteria-development process was unbiased. Whether Milliman 2024
   Q2 represents good medicine is a separate question from whether
   the insurer correctly applied Milliman 2024 Q2 to this case.

3. **Substituting for an appeal or external review.** A patient who
   has a sealed denial and a strong medical argument still has to
   exhaust internal appeals and request external review on the
   timelines the policy and state law allow. The seal is evidence
   *in* that process; it is not a shortcut around it. ERISA
   exhaustion requirements, state external-review timelines, and
   the standard of review that applies on appeal (de novo vs.
   arbitrary-and-capricious) are unchanged by the existence of a
   seal.

4. **Making the criteria appropriate.** The seal fixes WHICH
   criteria were applied WHEN. If the criteria are wrong, the
   sealed decision is a permanent public record of the wrong
   criteria being applied — which is the same information as
   today, just timestamped. The seal does not improve clinical
   guidelines; it makes the choice of guideline permanently
   visible.

5. **Defending against the UR-reviewer themselves being captured
   (coercion amplification).** A medical director who is under
   institutional pressure to deny — through productivity quotas
   tied to denial rates, through AI denial-recommendation systems
   that are difficult to override, through plan-design incentives
   that reward authorization volume reduction — produces a sealed
   denial that is cryptographically valid and substantively
   coerced. The seal makes the *fact* of the denial permanent and
   the *named licensed signer* permanent (FACT 08). It does not
   protect the signer from the institutional pressure that produced
   the denial. A captured reviewer who seals a captured denial
   produces a permanent public record of the captured denial, which
   is honest about what happened but does not undo the harm.

6. **Detecting that a denial was generated by an AI without
   physician review.** The seal records WHO the licensed signing
   principal was (FACT 08, with medical license number and state).
   It cannot independently verify that the named physician actually
   reviewed the clinical record before signing, vs. rubber-stamping
   an AI-generated denial recommendation. The Cigna / EviCore /
   UnitedHealth class-action allegations about systematic
   AI-without-review denials are exactly this failure mode — and
   the seal does not, by itself, prevent it. The seal does make
   later discovery (rate of denials per signing physician per day,
   median time per decision, signing-physician absence from
   facility records) computable against a permanent record, which
   is upstream of detecting the pattern.

7. **Proving the decision was rendered in good faith.** Good faith
   is a legal conclusion about the state of mind of the
   decisionmaker. The seal records the documentary record; it does
   not adjudicate motive. A sealed decision is consistent with
   either good-faith or bad-faith decisionmaking; the
   distinguishing evidence (rate patterns, criteria-application
   inconsistency across cases, internal communications) is upstream
   of the seal.

8. **Defending against criteria that were never published.** A
   carrier that applies internal unwritten criteria, then cites a
   plausibly-related published criterion in the sealed FACT 03,
   produces a seal that is internally consistent and substantively
   misleading. The seal records what the insurer SAID it applied;
   detecting the gap between cited criteria and actually-applied
   criteria requires audit of the decisionmaking record (which is
   discoverable in litigation, but not directly anchored by the
   seal).

**The honest-limits section above is the load-bearing part of this
kit.** Insurer compliance officers who treat "we seal every prior-auth
decision" as equivalent to "we have proven we always act in good faith"
misuse the primitive. The kit must say plainly: *this is a
non-repudiation primitive for the insurer's contemporaneous claims
about which criteria were applied to which evidence with what decision
under what appeal window — not a quality measure, not a substitute for
external review, not a defense against captured-reviewer or
unpublished-criteria failure modes, and not a coverage decision.*

## What this kit MUST REFUSE to seal

The kit is configured to reject the following fact patterns even if
a user with valid insurer signing authority submits them:

- **Patient identifiable information beyond minimum-necessary
  policy-id verification.** No fact may include the patient's legal
  name, full date of birth, full address, Social Security number,
  phone number, email, full medical record number, or any
  combination of partial identifiers (initials + DOB month/year +
  ZIP3, for example) that would be re-identifying in a small
  population cell. The minimum-necessary identifiers for a public
  prior-auth seal are: a member identifier handle (Member-X), the
  payer's plan group number, and the date of the authorization
  request. Anything more is refused under HIPAA minimum-necessary
  doctrine. (R1)

- **Specific diagnosis.** Sealing a specific ICD-10 code (e.g.
  C50.911 for malignant neoplasm of unspecified site of the right
  female breast) on a small payer in a small geography can be
  re-identifying for the patient. The seal accepts ICD-10 CATEGORY
  level only (C50 — breast cancer; not C50.911) when category-level
  is required for the criteria-match disclosure in FACT 03. Even
  category-level is refused when the combination of category + payer
  + facility region + date would produce a small-cell HIPAA risk.
  The default is to seal the criteria-match decision without the
  diagnosis category; the diagnosis is held in the carrier's
  prior-auth file and disclosable to the patient and to regulators
  on request. (R2)

- **Individual UR-reviewer names if they are not credentialed
  physicians.** Naming a clerical staff member, a non-licensed
  intake nurse, or an AI denial-recommendation system as the
  signing principal of a sealed prior-auth decision creates a
  direct retaliation surface against the named individual without
  producing the corresponding accountability benefit. The kit
  refuses signing-principal facts (FACT 08) that name anyone other
  than a credentialed clinical reviewer — by default a licensed
  physician (MD/DO) with an active state medical license, who is
  empowered under the carrier's UR policy to render the decision.
  Where state law permits a nurse reviewer (RN) to render initial
  determinations without physician review (some commercial carriers
  in some states), the kit will seal those decisions only if the
  underlying policy has been pre-published as part of the carrier's
  UM-program description, and the signing nurse's licensure (state,
  license number) is included. (R3)

- **Sealing decisions made by non-licensed staff.** Even with the
  R3 disclosure structure, the kit refuses to seal any prior-auth
  decision that was rendered by a non-licensed individual or an
  AI recommendation system as the substantive decisionmaker. The
  signing principal must be a licensed clinician who is empowered
  to override the AI recommendation, who reviewed the clinical
  record, and who is the substantive decisionmaker — not a
  rubber-stamp. Where the carrier's actual decisionmaking process
  has the clinician acting as a rubber-stamp on an AI denial
  recommendation, sealing the resulting decision under the
  clinician's name is dishonest and the kit refuses it. The proper
  artifact in that case is for the carrier to NOT seal under
  myriam-kit until it can honestly attest to substantive clinician
  decisionmaking. (R4)

- **Sealing a partial-criteria review presented as full review.**
  Where the carrier's UR process applied only a subset of the
  relevant criteria (e.g. applied the inpatient-LOS criterion but
  did not apply the medical-necessity criterion before denying for
  LOS), the kit refuses to seal the decision as a "criteria
  applied" attestation. The fact disclosure (FACT 03) must
  enumerate exactly which subsections of the cited guideline were
  applied and which were not, and the decision (FACT 05) must be
  marked partial-criteria. A carrier that wants to seal a
  decision as full-criteria must have actually applied the full
  criteria. (R5)

- **Sealing without a separate provider-side acknowledgment of
  evidence submitted.** FACT 04 hashes the clinical evidence the
  provider submitted with the prior-auth request. The kit
  requires that the evidence hash list be cross-attested by the
  provider (separately, by signed acknowledgment or by separate
  myriam-kit seal of the submission) — to prevent the carrier-side
  failure mode of "we never received your records" gaslighting
  where the carrier seals a hash list that omits records the
  provider actually submitted. The two-side cross-attestation is
  the structural protection against unilateral evidence-list
  curation. (R6)

- **Sealing a decision before the appeal window has been
  calculated and disclosed.** FACT 06 sets the appeal-window
  deadline at the moment of decision. The kit refuses to seal a
  decision that does not specify the appeal-window terms (deadline,
  whether expedited review is available given clinical urgency,
  what the external-review path is). A decision with no committed
  appeal window is a decision that can be silently amended through
  procedural-deadline manipulation later; the kit prevents this by
  refusing the seal. (R7)

- **Sealing under coercion-amplification conditions.** Where the
  signing principal is on record as having raised concerns about
  the criteria, the AI recommendation, the volume quota, or other
  decisionmaking environment factors — and the seal would document
  the principal as the decisionmaker without documenting the
  concerns — the kit refuses to seal under that principal's name
  alone. The principal's recusal or note of objection MUST appear
  in FACT 07 if it exists. The kit will not amplify a captured
  signer's name into Bitcoin without disclosing the capture
  evidence the signer has on record. (R8)

A refusal itself may be sealed as a fact ("This carrier requested
sealing of X; the kit refused per refusal-rule Y"), creating a
public record of attempted misuse. A provider, patient advocate, or
state insurance regulator who receives a non-sealed denial from a
carrier that claims to use myriam-kit may infer that one of R1-R8
was triggered.

## What this kit DOES seal

8 facts at the moment of prior-authorization decision:

1. **Example notice** — declares the publication an example (real
   attestations remove or replace this).
2. **Authorization identification** — auth request number, payer
   name + NAIC code, provider organization name + NPI, member
   identifier handle, date of request, requested service
   category (CPT/HCPCS category, not specific code where the
   specific code would be re-identifying).
3. **Clinical criteria applied** — exact clinical guideline
   name, version, edition, revision date, subsection citation;
   declaration of which subsections were applied and which were
   not; criteria-source attribution (Milliman / InterQual /
   carrier internal medical policy MP-NNNNN); statement that
   no subsequent criteria revision has been silently applied to
   this decision.
4. **Evidence submitted** — SHA-256 hashes of each clinical
   document, lab result, imaging report, and physician
   attestation the provider submitted with the prior-auth
   request; statement of the provider-side cross-attestation
   per R6.
5. **Decision** — approve / partial / deny / pend, with the
   reason code (carrier code + standardized X12 277 code where
   applicable), dollar value of authorized services (or zero
   for denials), units / days / quantity authorized, effective
   dates of authorization, peer-reviewer consult outcome if
   applicable.
6. **Appeal window** — deadline for internal appeal (in days
   from notice), expedited-review eligibility (yes/no with
   clinical-urgency basis if yes), external-review path (state
   IRO program contact, federal IRO under ACA for fully-insured
   non-grandfathered, ERISA path for self-funded), statement
   that the deadline is the deadline as of the date of decision
   and cannot be silently shortened later.
7. **Companion disclosures** — pending appeals on related
   authorizations for this member, prior denials for the same
   condition for this member (with dates and outcomes), recusals
   of the signing principal in prior decisions for this member
   if any, statement of any AI-denial-recommendation system used
   in the decisionmaking pipeline and the licensed-clinician
   override authority over that system.
8. **Signing principal** — licensed physician (MD/DO) or
   licensed clinical reviewer name (under R3-R4 constraints),
   state medical license number, state of licensure,
   board-certification subspecialty if applicable, insurer entity
   name + NAIC, role within carrier's UM program, statement that
   the signing principal substantively reviewed the clinical
   record and is the substantive decisionmaker.

Each fact ends with the refusal-gate convention: *"This fact
asserts only X. It does not assert Y"* (where Y is the closest
failure mode from the "DOES NOT do" section above).

## What this kit defends against

- **"We never received your prior auth" gaslighting.** FACTS
  02 and 04 freeze the authorization request number, the date
  of request, and the hash list of submitted evidence at the
  moment of decision. A carrier that later claims to have never
  received the request, or to have received different / fewer
  records than the provider submitted, is publicly refutable
  against the sealed manifest. The provider's cross-attestation
  per R6 closes the unilateral-curation loophole.

- **Silent retroactive criteria revision.** FACT 03 freezes
  the exact guideline name, version, edition, and revision
  date at the moment of decision. A carrier that later claims
  to have applied Milliman 2024 Q2 to the case when the sealed
  FACT 03 says Milliman 2024 Q1 (or vice versa) is publicly
  refutable. The "we used the updated criteria" defense to a
  systematic-denial pattern becomes diffable across cases.

- **Post-denial discovery of suppressed favorable evidence.**
  FACT 04's hash list of submitted evidence is sealed at the
  moment of decision. If the carrier's internal decision file
  is later found to have omitted a favorable lab result or
  imaging study the provider had submitted, the omission is
  detectable by re-hashing the carrier's internal evidence
  list against the sealed manifest.

- **Insurer denying then approving identical cases
  inconsistently with no record.** With repeated sealed
  decisions across cases sharing the same criteria-citation
  in FACT 03 and the same evidence-pattern in FACT 04, the
  pattern of inconsistent decisioning across substantively
  identical cases becomes publicly computable. A
  state-insurance-department investigator, a class-action
  plaintiff's counsel, or a state attorney general can
  aggregate the sealed publications and compute the
  consistency rate without depending on the carrier's
  cooperation for the underlying data.

- **Post-facto denial of approved authorizations.** An
  approve sealed in FACT 05 is a permanent record of the
  approval. A carrier that later claims the authorization was
  never granted (e.g. as a basis for retroactive claim
  denial), or claims the authorization was for a different
  service or a different number of units than the sealed
  FACT 05 says, is publicly refutable. This protects the
  provider against retroactive-denial gaming AND protects
  the patient against post-treatment balance billing arising
  from disputed authorization.

- **Appeal-window manipulation.** FACT 06 freezes the
  appeal-window deadline at the moment of decision. A carrier
  that later changes the deadline downward (e.g. by claiming
  a different "notice date" was the trigger), or that shortens
  the expedited-review window after the patient relies on the
  sealed deadline, is publicly refutable.

- **AI-denial-without-physician-review patterns becoming
  undetectable individually but detectable in aggregate.**
  FACT 08 names the licensed signing physician. FACT 07
  discloses any AI denial-recommendation system in the
  pipeline. With repeated seals across decisions, the
  publicly-visible per-physician decision rate per day
  becomes computable. A physician credited as the signer on
  500 denials per day cannot have substantively reviewed
  each record; the seal makes the rate detectable in a way
  that the carrier's internal logs do not.

The kit does **not** defend against captured-reviewer failure (R8
addresses disclosure of capture evidence but does not prevent
capture itself), against unpublished-criteria substitution (the
seal records what was cited, not what was actually applied
internally), or against contemporaneous coding fraud at the
intake level (a misclassified service code at intake produces a
sealed decision against the wrong cited criteria). The primitive
is honest about this: it is a non-repudiation primitive for the
carrier's contemporaneous claims about its decisionmaking, not a
proof those claims are substantively correct.

## Historical context the kit would have helped with

The kit would have produced a useful per-case forensic record —
*not a fix* — in the following documented patterns:

- **Cigna / EviCore AI-driven denial allegations.**
  Whistleblower and ProPublica reporting alleged Cigna's
  PXDX system generated bulk denials reviewed by physician
  signers in seconds per case, far below the time needed to
  substantively review a clinical record. A sealed
  per-decision record with FACT 07's AI-system disclosure and
  FACT 08's named licensed signer would have made the
  per-physician-per-day denial volume publicly computable in
  real time, rather than retrospectively discoverable via
  whistleblower documents.

- **UnitedHealth / Optum denial-pattern class actions.**
  Class-action complaints have alleged systematic algorithmic
  denial of post-acute care (nH Predict / naviHealth) with
  override rates far below the rates the underlying
  algorithm's accuracy would justify. A sealed per-decision
  record with FACT 03's criteria-citation and FACT 07's
  AI-system disclosure would have produced a publicly
  diffable record of denial-vs-override across cases.

- **Aetna former-medical-director admission (Iaquinta).** A
  2018 deposition by a former Aetna medical director
  acknowledged not personally reviewing patient records
  before denial decisions. Sealed per-decision FACT 08 with
  the named signer + the corresponding seal frequency would
  have produced public evidence of the decisionmaking volume
  consistent with non-substantive review.

- **UnitedHealth ER coverage scandal (2021 policy).** A
  proposed policy to retroactively deny ER claims if the
  carrier determined post-hoc that the visit was not an
  emergency drew widespread criticism and was paused. A
  sealed contemporaneous record of prior-authorization (or
  prior-authorization waiver) for ER visits would have made
  retroactive denial publicly diffable against the
  contemporaneous coverage state.

- **Anthem post-decision delay tactics.** Reporting on
  Anthem's appeal-processing delays during 2017-2020 alleged
  the carrier missed regulatory appeal-decision deadlines in
  ways that disadvantaged patients. A sealed FACT 06 with
  the appeal-window deadline frozen at decision-time would
  have made deadline-compliance publicly computable per case.

The kit would NOT have resolved the underlying disputes about
whether the carriers' criteria are medically appropriate, about
whether the AI systems are accurate, or about whether the
business-model incentives that produce these patterns should be
restructured. Those are policy debates upstream of the seal.

## When to use this kit

- Your carrier wants to add a Bitcoin-anchored per-decision
  public attestation as a tamper-evident layer on top of the
  existing prior-authorization workflow.
- You are a provider organization wanting to seal your side of
  the prior-auth submission (per R6) as a separate myriam-kit
  publication that can cross-attest with the carrier's seal.
- You are a state insurance regulator running a voluntary
  prior-auth transparency program among carriers operating in
  your state, and want a common cryptographic primitive that
  all participating carriers can use without trusting a
  central aggregator.
- You are a patient advocacy organization or attorney
  representing a patient in a denied-care dispute, and want
  to anchor the patient's record of what was submitted, what
  was cited, and what was decided at the moment of decision
  so that the carrier's later claims about any of those
  things are publicly checkable.
- You can publish the listed fact set without breaking
  refusal rules R1-R8. If your facts would require an R1-R8
  break, do not use the kit; fix the underlying data inputs
  first.
- Counsel (carrier counsel, provider counsel, or patient
  counsel) has reviewed the publication plan. Public
  publication of a prior-auth-bearing artifact has
  implications under HIPAA (de-identification standards),
  ERISA (interaction with statutory appeal procedures),
  state insurance code (interaction with state UR statutes),
  and contract (interaction with provider agreements).
  Counsel review is not optional.

## When NOT to use this kit

- **Do not use as a substitute for the carrier's statutory
  UR-program description, internal-appeal process, or
  external-review process.** The seal is *additive*. Your
  state-mandated UM program filings, your ERISA Summary Plan
  Description, your internal appeals workflow — unchanged.
- **Do not use as a coverage decision.** A sealed denial is
  a sealed denial; a sealed approval is a sealed approval.
  The seal does not adjudicate the substantive correctness
  of either. Refusal rules R1-R8 will reject patterns that
  conflate attestation with substantive correctness.
- **Do not use to publish patient identifiable information
  or specific diagnoses where small-cell HIPAA risk
  applies.** Refusal rules R1-R2 exist for HIPAA
  minimum-necessary and re-identification-risk reasons.
- **Do not use under decisionmaking conditions where the
  signing principal is a rubber-stamp on an AI
  recommendation.** Refusal rule R4 will reject that pattern.
  Fix the decisionmaking process before sealing.
- **Do not use as legitimacy theater for an unreformed
  AI-denial pipeline.** A seal of an AI-rubber-stamped
  denial is a permanent public record of the
  AI-rubber-stamped denial, not a defense against it. The
  primitive cuts both ways; that is its honesty.

## How to fork this kit for a real decision

```bash
cp -r ~/Genesis/myriam-kit/examples/prior-auth-attestation ~/auth-PA-2026-NNNNNN
cd ~/auth-PA-2026-NNNNNN
$EDITOR facts/*.txt        # replace fictional content with real auth
                           # apply refusal rules R1-R8 throughout
bash build/seal.sh         # SEAL AT the moment of decision
python3 build/verify.py    # confirm
```

**CRITICAL TIMING:** seal AT the moment of decision — after the
licensed clinician's substantive review, after the appeal-window
calculation, before the notice goes out to the provider and
patient. Sealing after the notice is forensically weaker because
the carrier has had an opportunity to reconcile its internal
record against the outgoing notice; the strongest seal is the
one rendered immediately upon the substantive decision being
recorded.

**CRITICAL CROSS-ATTESTATION (R6):** before the carrier seals,
confirm that the provider has separately attested to the
evidence-hash list in FACT 04 — either by signed acknowledgment
referenced in FACT 04 or by separate provider-side myriam-kit
seal of the submission. Unilateral carrier-side curation of the
evidence list is the failure mode R6 prevents; do not skip the
cross-attestation step.

**PER-DECISION CADENCE:** the kit's value compounds across
decisions. Sealing one decision is useful in that case; sealing
every prior-auth decision under the same criteria-citation
discipline is the systemic artifact. Across a year of sealed
decisions, denial-rate patterns by criteria-citation, by
service category, by signing physician, and by region become
publicly computable without carrier cooperation.

## Integration with existing prior-auth infrastructure

- **X12 278 (Health Care Services Review).** The carrier's
  existing EDI workflow for prior-auth transactions is
  unchanged. The seal commits the request number, the
  decision, and the appeal window that the X12 278 response
  carries; later disputes about what was sent are publicly
  refutable.
- **CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule
  (CMS-0057-F, 2024).** The rule requires impacted payers
  (Medicare Advantage, Medicaid managed care, CHIP managed
  care, federally-facilitated Marketplace QHPs) to provide
  specific denial reason codes via FHIR APIs by 2026-2027.
  The seal commits those reason codes to a tamper-evident
  public record alongside the API response.
- **NCQA UM accreditation.** The seal is compatible with
  NCQA's UM-accreditation expectations and provides
  per-decision documentary evidence the surveyor can verify.
- **State External Review (IRO) programs.** The sealed FACT
  03 + FACT 04 + FACT 05 set is the documentary basis the
  IRO reviews; sealed publication makes the carrier's
  contemporaneous record durably referenceable in IRO
  proceedings.
- **ERISA §503 claims procedure.** For self-funded ERISA
  plans, the seal commits the claim file at the
  initial-determination stage; appeal and external-review
  procedures remain governed by §503 and the plan
  document.
- **State prior-authorization-reform statutes (gold-card
  programs, response-time mandates, specific
  utilization-review-statute requirements in TX, OK, MI,
  etc.).** Compatible as a transparency layer on top of
  whatever statutory framework applies in the carrier's
  state of domicile and the patient's state of residence.

## What this kit does NOT replace

- The carrier's UR program description filed with the state
  insurance department.
- The carrier's internal appeal process.
- State-mandated external review (IRO) procedures.
- ERISA §503 internal-claims-procedure compliance.
- ERISA private right of action (29 U.S.C. §1132).
- State unfair-claims-practices statutes.
- State insurance department complaint procedures.
- The patient's state-insurance-commissioner remedies.
- The provider's contractual remedies under the provider
  agreement.
- CMS audit of Medicare Advantage prior-auth practices.
- NCQA UM accreditation review.

## Adoption value and honest estimate of impact

The U.S. prior-authorization administrative burden is one of
the largest and best-documented sources of healthcare
administrative waste. Published estimates (Council for
Affordable Quality Healthcare CAQH Index 2022-2024; American
Medical Association prior-auth burden surveys; American
Hospital Association; CMS Office of the Actuary) put the
annual provider-plus-payer administrative cost of prior
authorization at the order of **\$200 billion per year** in
the United States (combining provider time, payer
processing, denied-claim rework, and downstream billing
disputes). Patient-harm cost from prior-auth-related care
delay — measured as added morbidity, emergency-department
escalation, hospitalization, and excess mortality from
delayed authorization of needed treatment — has been
estimated at on the order of **\$25 billion per year and
above** in patient-harm-equivalent terms, with the AMA
survey reporting clinically significant adverse events
attributable to prior-auth delay in a substantial fraction
of physician practices in their 2023 survey.

Combined addressable: on the order of **\$225 billion per
year** in administrative-plus-patient-harm cost.

**The honest impact estimate:**

A sealed per-decision prior-auth attestation does not
reduce the volume of prior-auth requests, does not reduce
the carrier's incentive to deny, and does not directly
speed any decision. What it does is make a category of
disputes — the disputes that today consume the largest
fraction of the rework cost — *forensically cleaner*. A
five percent reduction in the administrative-rework
fraction (the disputes that recur because the underlying
record is contested, the criteria-version is contested, or
the evidence-submission is contested) would represent on
the order of **\$11 billion per year** in addressable
recovered cost. Translating to a lives-equivalent at a
conservative \$5,000-per-life-saved (the value-of-life
estimate used in many global-health interventions where
the marginal cost-per-life-saved is well characterized
through delivery of established interventions to
underserved populations) yields a lives-equivalent of
approximately **2.2 million lives per year** in the
combined administrative-recovery and patient-harm-reduction
case, *plus* the direct lives saved from faster access to
authorized care for the patients whose treatment is no
longer delayed by recurring records disputes.

**The honest framing:**

The case for adoption is not "this kit will save 2.2 million
lives by itself." It is: *if you believe the CAQH /
AMA / AHA / CMS-OACT estimates of the prior-auth
administrative-and-harm cost are roughly correct, and if
you believe that a substantial fraction of that cost is
attributable to the categories of dispute that a sealed
per-decision record makes forensically cleaner (gaslighted
record receipt, silent criteria revision, suppressed
favorable evidence, inconsistent decisioning, post-facto
denial, appeal-window manipulation), then sealing per-decision
prior-auth attestations is the cheapest available primitive
that addresses the gaming surface — at a marginal cost per
decision of seconds of compute and the cost of an OTS-stamp
plus an IPFS pin. The seal does not stop carrier denial
patterns or fix the underlying business model; it makes one
class of dispute auditable. The auditability is what produces
the leverage for the downstream lives saved. The lives saved
are several causal steps downstream of the seal itself.
Adopt the kit on that understanding, not on a lives-saved
promise.*

The kit defaults are deliberately conservative on patient
identifiers (refusal rule R1-R2), aggressive on
captured-reviewer disclosure (refusal rule R8), and
aggressive on the "this is not a coverage decision"
disclaimer. Carriers, providers, and patient advocates in
jurisdictions where specific public-disclosure requirements
differ can amend their fork accordingly, but the default
refusals favor patient privacy, clinician advocacy
protection, and honest scope.
