WHY THE PRINCIPAL IS ROLE-LEVEL, NOT PERSON-LEVEL

In real-world law-enforcement practice, the binding between an
agency attestation and the specific persons who produced it is
established through:
  - the agency's own personnel records and assignment rosters,
  - the agency's internal-affairs records of who held which role
    on which date,
  - the formal subpoena, discovery, or oversight processes that
    are equipped to compel role-to-individual binding under
    access-controlled review,
  - the live testimony of role-holders at any subsequent
    proceeding.

A public Bitcoin-anchored seal is not the appropriate venue to bind
a sergeant's or IA officer's personal identity to a case. That
binding belongs in the agency record where access is controlled and
where the binding can be tested through cross-examination, IA
internal review, civilian-oversight subpoena, or court process. The
role-level public seal plus the separately-discoverable role-to-
individual binding (hashed in FACT 05) is the kit's default;
agencies in jurisdictions where public personal-naming is required
can amend their fork to include the personal name, but the default
refusal favors signer safety and whistleblower protection.

In a real attestation, the signing principal section should include:

  - The agency's identity, ORI, and statutory basis.
  - The role and supervisor-role for each signatory.
  - A cryptographic key (PGP, X.509, agency-issued) for each
    signatory so that future statements claimed to be from either
    signing principal can be cryptographically verified against
    this seal.
  - The incident, intake-completion, and sealing timestamps.
  - The third-signer field if federal monitoring or court-ordered
    receivership requires it.

The signing principal section SHOULD NOT include:

  - Personal contact information for either signatory.
  - Home addresses or other locating information.
  - Identifying information about either signatory's family
    members.
  - The personal name of any private party to the incident
    (suspect, complainant, victim, witness, bystander,
    informant).
  - Per-officer personal naming of involved officers beyond the
    role-level identification in FACT 05.

This fact asserts only the signing principal identification fields
for the fictional incident as declared, with both required
signatories (supervising sergeant and IA officer) at the role
level, and the recommendation-but-not-requirement of a third
federal-monitor signer. It does not assert any of those fields
about any real agency, any real officer, or any real incident. It
does not assert that the two-signer model used in this fictional
example would be sufficient in any specific real-world incident
where federal monitoring or other oversight requirements may
impose a higher signer-count floor.
