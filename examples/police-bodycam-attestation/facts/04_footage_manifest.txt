The following table lists the per-file SHA-256 hashes of every video
segment, audio-only segment, and metadata sidecar file ingested into
the agency's digital evidence-management system (DEMS) for the
incident defined in FACT 02. The actual file contents are NOT
included in this MYRIAM publication — only their cryptographic
hashes. This is the core chain-of-custody property of the seal: the
manifest anchors WHICH BYTES the agency holds, WITHOUT exposing the
contents of the footage to the public.

WHY PER-FILE HASHES (NOT JUST ONE WRAPPER HASH)

The seal lists per-segment hashes rather than a single combined-
archive hash because the operational gaming pattern this seal
defends against is the silent removal of individual segments
(individual BWC angles, individual short clips within a longer
recording, individual audio tracks) from a later production. A
combined-archive hash would catch deletion of the whole archive but
not silent removal of one segment within the archive. Per-segment
hashing makes individual-segment removal cryptographically
detectable.

REDACTED-BYSTANDER VERSIONS (referenced in this manifest at the
redacted hash; the unredacted versions are NOT in the public seal
per refusal rule R3 — see FACT 06 for the redaction event log)

For any segment in the manifest above that contains visible
bystander faces (per refusal rule R3, bystanders are anyone NOT
party to the incident — e.g. uninvolved pedestrians, residents
behind windows, drivers of passing vehicles), a redacted version is
the canonical PUBLIC-FACING file. The unredacted source remains in
the agency's DEMS under the agency's retention policy. The redacted
hash appears in the manifest above; the unredacted hash is sealed
SEPARATELY in the redaction log (FACT 06) for chain-of-custody
purposes (so that the redaction event itself is tamper-evident) but
is not part of this public manifest.

In this fictional example, segments 1, 3, and 8 contain bystander
faces and are listed with their REDACTED hashes; the unredacted
hashes appear in FACT 06.

WHAT IS INTENTIONALLY ABSENT FROM THIS MANIFEST

  - Per-frame hashes inside any video segment. The seal anchors
    file-level integrity; frame-level integrity is part of any
    forensic-video examination report and is sealed separately if
    needed.
  - Hashes of any privately-recorded video (e.g. cellphone footage
    of the incident captured by a bystander or by a non-agency
    party). Privately-recorded footage is not in agency custody
    and is not the agency's to seal; a complainant or other party
    may seal their own footage in a separate myriam-kit publication.
  - Hashes of recordings made by other agencies' devices that may
    have been present (e.g. another agency's officers responding
    as backup). Other-agency footage is sealed by the other
    agency in their own myriam-kit publication if they choose to;
    FACT 05's custody log records any inter-agency hand-offs.
  - Personally-identifying metadata in filenames. The filename
    convention above uses anonymized device IDs and the incident
    number only; the filename is not a re-identifying string.

VERIFICATION PROCEDURE

  Any party — defense counsel, civil-rights plaintiff's counsel, IA
  investigator, civilian-oversight board member, federal monitor,
  court-appointed neutral, journalist with proper legal access —
  receiving any of the above files through a lawful release
  mechanism (FOIA, court order, oversight subpoena, etc.) may re-
  hash the file they receive with SHA-256 and compare to the row
  above. If the hashes match, the recipient holds the exact bytes
  that were sealed at the moment of ingest. If the hashes do not
  match, the file has been altered (intentionally or accidentally)
  since ingest; the recipient should raise this immediately with
  the issuing agency, opposing counsel where applicable, and any
  oversight authority with jurisdiction.

  A produced manifest that contains FEWER files than the sealed
  manifest is publicly detectable: the absent file is enumerable by
  its row above. A produced manifest that contains MORE files
  raises a separate question (where did the additional footage
  come from, and why wasn't it sealed) that the agency must
  account for; the seal does not foreclose the additional footage
  being legitimate, only requires that its provenance be
  explained.

In this fictional example, the hashes above are deterministic
placeholders. A real intake would replace these with hashes of the
actual files ingested.

This fact asserts only the manifest of file-level SHA-256 hashes,
filenames, sizes, and durations as ingested into the DEMS at the
intake-completion moment for the incident in FACT 02. It does not
assert:
  - that the footage contents depict any specific event,
  - whether any officer or other party's conduct in the footage
    was lawful, proper, within policy, criminal, or actionable,
  - that the manifest is complete with respect to all recordings
    that existed of the incident (other-party recordings are out
    of scope; the seal commits only to agency-custody recordings),
  - that any individual file is admissible under any specific
    court's evidentiary standard,
  - that any redaction applied to a segment was appropriate
    (covered separately in FACT 06).
